1 Kiln & motor

Typical cement kilns: 3–6 m diameter. Used to convert pulses/rev into mm of shell travel per pulse.
Kiln reference speed is the design/nameplate speed that pairs with motor speed to fix the drivetrain ratio — it's a hardware constant. Typical normal-operation kiln speed is 2–5 rpm; actual running speed can vary (e.g. via VFD) without changing this ratio.
Used only for the worst-case pulse-frequency check (resolution itself doesn't change with kiln speed).

2 Final gear ratio

The girth gear ring bolted to the kiln itself has no room for an encoder — this mesh is always the last, inaccessible leg to the kiln. Everything on the pinion-wheel side of it (points A and B) turns at this same ratio relative to the kiln.

3 Encoder target

Quadrature decoding (×4 — counting both edges of both A/B channels) is assumed throughout; it's the standard way these counters are wired, so it isn't a toggle here.
Check your counter card / PLC input spec. High-PPR encoders on the fast motor shaft can exceed this even when the resolution looks fine.

How this is calculated

The gear ratio between a shaft and the kiln is fixed by hardware, found once from motor speed ÷ kiln reference speed (point C) or the pinion:girth ratio alone (points A/B) — independent of how fast the kiln actually runs later. Pulses per kiln revolution = PPR × shaft revs per kiln rev × 4 (quadrature). Resolution = π × kiln diameter ÷ pulses per kiln revolution. Frequency uses the max operating kiln speed to catch worst-case counter overload.

Why 20,000–100,000 pulses/rev?

Too few pulses gives coarse shell position resolution; too many risks exceeding counter/interface frequency limits for little practical benefit. On a 5 m kiln, 20,000 pulses/rev ≈ 0.79 mm resolution and 100,000 ≈ 0.16 mm — both fine, so pick whichever standard encoder is cheapest/most available for the mounting point you actually have room for.
